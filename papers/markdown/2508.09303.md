## Abstract

Reasoning-augmented search agents such as Search-R1, trained via reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR), demonstrate remarkable capabilities in multi-step information retrieval from external knowledge sources. These agents address the limitations of their parametric memory by dynamically gathering relevant facts to address complex reasoning tasks. However, existing approaches suffer from a fundamental architectural limitation: they process search queries strictly sequentially, even when handling inherently parallelizable and logically independent comparisons. This sequential bottleneck significantly constrains computational efficiency, particularly for queries that require multiple entity comparisons. To address this critical limitation, we propose ParallelSearch , a novel reinforcement learning framework that empowers large language models (LLMs) to recognize parallelizable query structures and execute multiple search operations concurrently. Our approach introduces dedicated reward functions that incentivize the identification of independent query components while preserving answer accuracy through jointly considering correctness, query decomposition quality, and parallel execution benefits. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate that ParallelSearch outperforms state-ofthe-art baselines by an average performance gain of 2 . 9% across seven question-answering benchmarks. Notably, on parallelizable questions, our method achieves a 12 . 7% performance improvement while requiring only 69 . 6% of the LLM calls compared to sequential approaches. ## 1. Introduction

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional proficiency in complex reasoning tasks (Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024), yet remain fundamentally constrained by their static parametric knowledge, which cannot access real-time information or domain-specific facts beyond their training cutoff (Gao et al., 2023). To address this limitation, recent advances, like Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025b), in reasoning-augmented search agents have emerged, augmenting LLMs with multi-step information retrieval capabilities that enable dynamic knowledge acquisition from external sources through reinforcement learning to strategically formulate search queries, decompose complex questions, and synthesize retrieved information into coherent responses.

However, despite these advances, current reasoning-augmented search agents suffer from a critical architectural inefficiency: they process all queries through a strictly sequential pipeline, treating each search operation as inherently dependent on its predecessors (Jin et al., 2025b; Sun et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025a). This sequential bottleneck persists even when handling parallelizable queries that contain logically independent components, as shown in Fig. 1. This workflow requires three LLM calls and two sequential search operations, resulting in computational overhead and latency. In production environments serving millions of queries, such inefficiencies cascade into prohibitive computational costs and degraded user experiences (Al-Maskari and Sanderson, 2010), particularly as agents tackle complex tasks requiring multiple steps.

Motivated by these limitations, our key insight is that many complex queries can be naturally decomposed into independent sub-components, which can be searched concurrently without compromising reasoning quality. In the aforementioned example, retrieving birth dates for Claude Monet and Camille Pissarro involves no logical dependencies, and these operations can and should be executed in parallel. Therefore, we propose ParallelSearch , a novel reinforcement learning framework, to train LLMs to recognize such parallelizable patterns, decompose queries into independent subqueries within a single reasoning step, execute these subqueries concurrently, and synthesize results efficiently. This approach reduces the number of required LLM calls and enables parallel search execution, resulting in a significant reduction in both inference time and costs.

Our framework leverages Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (Guo et al., 2025; Schulman et al., 2017) to teach LLMs when and how to identify parallelizable query structures, generate multiple sub-queries in a single pass, and aggregate results effectively. The primary technical challenge lies in training models to accurately detect query independence while avoiding over-parallelization that could introduce redundant searches or miss critical dependencies. We address this through two novel reward components that complement the standard answer correctness reward and format reward: (1) a decomposition reward that explicitly incentivizes correct identification of parallelizable patterns, and (2) a search efficiency reward that encourages single-round completion for parallelizable queries while penalizing unnecessary sequential processing.

The main contributions of our work are as follows:

- We identify and formalize the parallel query execution problem in reasoning-augmented search agents,

demonstrating through empirical analysis that current sequential processing paradigms incur substantial computational overhead for parallelizable queries.

- We propose ParallelSearch , a reinforcement learning framework employing verifiable rewards to train LLMs to recognize parallelizable queries, decompose complex questions into independent sub-queries, and execute concurrent searches while maintaining answer accuracy.
- We demonstrate through extensive experiments across seven question-answering benchmarks that our method achieves a 2 . 9% average performance improvement over SOTA baselines, with particularly notable gains of 12 . 7% on parallelizable questions, while reducing LLM calls by 30 . 4% and maintaining robust generalization to out-of-domain datasets.

## 2. Related Work

## 2.1. Deep Search Agents

While Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit impressive reasoning abilities (Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024), they suffer from insufficient domain-specific knowledge (Li et al., 2023) and hallucination issues (Ji et al., 2023). To incorporate external data sources, a retriever can be integrated to provide external information to LLMs through two main approaches, including retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)(Guu et al., 2020) and utilizing retrievers as tools (Schick et al., 2023). The RAG approaches (Gao et al., 2023) generally employ a retrieval phase followed by generation, wherein a retriever returns relevant content based on the input query. Nevertheless, this method can encounter difficulties with fetching irrelevant content (Jin et al., 2025a). The search-as-a-tool paradigm offers an alternative, enabling LLMs to engage with retrievers through prompting or fine-tuning. Methods like IRCoT (Trivedi et al., 2023) and ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) employ prompting for iterative reasoning combined with retriever queries, whereas Toolformer (Schick et al., 2023) uses supervised fine-tuning to improve search functionality. Recent research, like Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025b), employs reinforcement learning (Guo et al., 2025) to train LLMs as agents for search tasks, achieving promising results. Follow-ups, like ZeroSearch (Sun et al., 2025), O 2 -Searcher (Mei et al., 2025), MaskSearch (Wu et al., 2025b), R-Search Zhao et al. (2025), and StepSearch Wang et al. (2025b), further define fine-grained reward functions to improve the search performance. Despite these advances, existing search agents execute queries in a strictly sequential manner. Our ParallelSearch recognizes parallelizable query patterns, decomposes complex questions into independent sub-queries, and executes concurrent searches while maintaining accuracy.

## 2.2. Reinforcement Learning

Reinforcement learning (RL) constitutes a decision-making paradigm where agents maximize long-term rewards by learning from environmental interactions and feedback signals (Sutton and Barto, 1998). The application of RL to LLM fine-tuning began with Ouyang et al. (2022), who introduced RL from human feedback (RLHF) (Kaufmann et al., 2023). In RLHF, human preference data (Lambert et al., 2025) serves to construct a reward model that subsequently guides policy LLM optimization, predominantly through Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017). The computational demands of PPO, which necessitates iterative LLM updates, pose substantial practical barriers. This has motivated the development of streamlined alternatives such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), SimPO (Meng et al., 2024), and ORPO (Hong et al., 2024), which bypass explicit reward modeling. While these methods reduce computational overhead, they struggle with off-policy limitations (Pang et al., 2024) and often underperform relative to standard RL techniques. Novel solutions have emerged, including Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Guo et al., 2025), which leverages group-based scoring to eliminate critic networks alongside RLOO (Ahmadian et al., 2024). The potential of RL for enhancing LLM capabilities in search engine utilization and reasoning tasks has received increasing attention. Our work extends verifiable rewards to parallel search planning, enabling RL training for learning complex parallelization patterns and addressing a critical gap where previous verifiable reward mechanisms cannot assess execution efficiency or dependency reasoning.

## 4. Results and Analysis

## 4.1. Experimental Setup

Datasets and Evaluation Metrics We select seven benchmark datasets that encompass a diverse range of search with reasoning challenges. These datasets are categorized as follows: (1) General Question Answering: NQ (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), and PopQA (Mallen et al., 2023). (2) Multi-Hop Question Answering: HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), 2WikiMultiHopQA (Ho et al., 2020), Musique (Trivedi et al., 2022), and Bamboogle (Press et al., 2023). Following Jin et al. (2025b), we merge the training sets of NQ and HotpotQA as the training data and conduct evaluations on the validation or test sets, using Exact Match (EM) as the evaluation metric to assess both in-domain and out-of-domain performance.

Baseline Methods We employ various baselines to evaluate our proposed ParallelSearch , including R1 without search engine (Guo et al., 2025), Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025b), ZeroSearch (Sun et al., 2025), StepSearch (Wang et al., 2025b), and OTC (Wang et al., 2025a).

Implementation Details We conduct experiments using Qwen-2.5-7B (Base/Instruct) models (Yang et al., 2024) as the backbone of the search agent, E5 (Wang et al., 2022) as the embedding model, and 2018 Wikipedia dump (Karpukhin et al., 2020) as the corpus. We set the number of retrieved passages to 3 across all retrieval-based methods following Jin et al. (2025b). All experiments are conducted on 8 NVIDIA H100 GPUs. Detailed parameters used in experiments can be found in App. A.4.

## 4.4. Reward Design Analysis

Reward function design is important. Recent work (Shao et al., 2025) demonstrates that LLMs can still be improved, even when provided with noisy or even random rewards. Wu et al. (2025a) suggests that this is likely due to a data contamination issue, which Qwen models may encounter during pre-training, leading to mathematical and coding problems. In our experiments, we find that the design of the reward function is critical and can significantly affect performance. We argue that this is due to the multi-turn interaction with LLMs, such as search, which is not pre-trained. As shown in Tab. 4, 𝜆 𝑑 = 0 increases the number of turns and decreases the decomposition ratio. Additionally, 𝜆 𝑠 = 0 significantly increases the number of turns, showing that these scores effectively decompose the parallelizable questions and prevent excessive search. When setting 𝜆 𝑑 to 0 . 15 and 𝜆 𝑠 to 0 . 35 , ParallelSearch achieves the best performance.

𝑟 𝑑 and 𝑟 𝑠 reduce the number of valid searches and response length. As shown in Fig. 5 (a) and (b), our reward function maintains a lower number of valid searches and response length, although both are slightly increased to better gather and extract useful information. Note that the length of retrieval documents is included in the response length here. From Fig. 5 (c), our analysis reveals that the instruction-tuned model exhibits accelerated convergence and demonstrates superior initial training rewards. Nevertheless, both model variants achieve remarkably similar final reward values upon completion of training. The results indicate that reinforcement learning serves as an effective method for closing this performance differential over the training, ultimately allowing base models to attain equivalent levels of performance.

## 5. Conclusion

We presented ParallelSearch , a novel reinforcement learning framework that addresses the critical efficiency bottleneck in reasoning-augmented search agents. By training LLMs to recognize and exploit query parallelizability , our approach enables concurrent execution of independent sub-queries while maintaining answer accuracy. Through carefully designed reward functions that jointly optimize for correctness, decomposition quality, and search efficiency, our method achieves a 2 . 9% average performance improvement across seven benchmarks, with particularly notable gains of 12 . 7% on parallelizable questions while reducing LLM calls by 30 . 4% . Our method demonstrates that architectural improvements in search agents need not come at the cost of additional model parameters or training data. Instead, teaching models when and how to parallelize through reinforcement learning offers a promising path toward more efficient and scalable information retrieval systems.

## A. Experiment Setup

## A.1. Datasets

We select seven benchmark datasets that encompass a diverse range of search with reasoning challenges, including (1) General Question Answering: NQ (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), and PopQA (Mallen et al., 2023). (2) Multi-Hop Question Answering: HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), 2WikiMultiHopQA (Ho et al., 2020), Musique (Trivedi et al., 2022), and Bamboogle (Press et al., 2023). We extraly introduce MultiHopRAG (Tang and Yang, 2024) to evaluate the performance using parallelizable and sequential questions. For training, we merge the training sets of NQ and HotpotQA to construct a dataset for ParallelSearch and other fine-tuning based baselines.

Natural Questions (NQ) is a large-scale open-domain question answering dataset designed to advance natural language understanding capabilities. The dataset comprises 307 K training examples, 8 K development examples, and 8 K test examples, all derived from Wikipedia pages presented to human annotators. NQ supports two distinct tasks: long answer selection, where systems must identify relevant passages, and short answer selection, which requires the extraction of specific answer spans. Human performance benchmarks have been established at 87% F1 for long-answer selection and 76% F1 for short-answer selection, setting challenging targets for computational models. The dataset is available in two formats: a comprehensive 42 GB version containing full HTML content, and a simplified 4 GB version with extracted text for the training set. Released under the Creative Commons Share-Alike 3.0 license, Natural Questions represents a significant resource for researchers working on reading comprehension and question answering systems, with the explicit goal of developing models capable of matching human-level understanding.

TriviaQA is a large-scale reading comprehension dataset comprising over 65 K question-answer-evidence triples, designed to challenge existing question-answering systems. The dataset features 95 K question-answer pairs originally authored by trivia enthusiasts, paired with independently gathered evidence documents averaging six per question, providing high-quality distant supervision. TriviaQA distinguishes itself from other reading comprehension datasets through three key characteristics: (1) relatively complex, compositional questions that require sophisticated understanding, (2) considerable syntactic and lexical variability between questions and their corresponding answer-evidence sentences, and (3) the necessity for cross-sentence reasoning to identify correct answers. Initial baseline evaluations reveal a substantial performance gap, with a feature-based classifier achieving 23% accuracy and state-of-the-art neural networks reaching 40% , both significantly below the 80% benchmark for human performance. This considerable margin underscores TriviaQA's value as a challenging testbed for advancing research in reading comprehension and question answering.

PopQA is a large-scale, open-domain question-answering dataset specifically designed to evaluate language models' ability to memorize and recall factual knowledge across varying levels of entity popularity. The dataset comprises 14 K entity-centric QA pairs systematically generated by converting knowledge tuples from Wikidata into natural language questions using carefully crafted templates. Each question is derived from one of 16 diverse relationship types and includes comprehensive annotations of the subject entity, object entity, and relationship type, along with Wikipedia monthly page view statistics to quantify entity popularity. Unlike existing QA datasets that predominantly feature high-popularity entities, PopQA intentionally encompasses entities across the popularity spectrum, with particular emphasis on long-tail entities. This design enables fine-grained analysis of how well language models encode world knowledge in their parameters, revealing that while scaling improves memorization of popular knowledge, models continue to struggle with less popular factual information, necessitating retrieval augmentation for effective performance on long-tail entities.

HotpotQA is a comprehensive question answering dataset containing 113 K Wikipedia-based question-answer pairs designed to advance complex reasoning capabilities in QA systems. The dataset addresses critical limitations in existing QA benchmarks through four distinctive features: (1) questions that necessitate finding and reasoning over multiple supporting documents, requiring genuine multi-hop reasoning; (2) diverse questions

unconstrained by pre-existing knowledge bases or schemas, ensuring broad coverage of reasoning patterns; (3) sentence-level annotations of supporting facts that enable strong supervision during training and facilitate explainable predictions; and (4) a novel category of factoid comparison questions that evaluate systems' abilities to extract relevant facts and perform comparative analysis. Unlike traditional single-hop QA datasets, HotpotQA challenges models to perform sophisticated reasoning chains while maintaining interpretability through the provision of explicit supporting evidence. The provision of sentence-level supporting facts represents a significant advancement, enabling QA systems not only to arrive at correct answers but also to demonstrate their reasoning process, thereby addressing the growing need for explainable AI in question-answering tasks.

2WikiMultiHopQA is a multi-hop question answering dataset comprising 192 , 606 examples that addresses critical limitations in existing multi-hop QA benchmarks by guaranteeing genuine multi-hop reasoning requirements. The dataset uniquely combines structured data from Wikidata and unstructured text from Wikipedia summaries, leveraging logical rules and carefully designed templates to generate four distinct question types: comparison (requiring quantitative or logical comparisons), inference (requiring understanding of logical rules), compositional (requiring sequential answering of sub-questions), and bridge comparison (combining bridge entity identification with comparative reasoning). A distinguishing feature of 2WikiMultiHopQA is the inclusion of evidence information, which provides complete reasoning paths from question to answer, serving the dual purposes of enabling comprehensive explanations for model predictions and facilitating rigorous evaluation of reasoning capabilities. The dataset exhibits diverse answer types, with the top categories being yes/no ( 31 . 2% ), dates ( 16 . 9% ), films ( 13 . 5% ), humans ( 11 . 7% ), and cities ( 4 . 7% ), encompassing a total of 708 unique answer types. Through its methodical construction pipeline, 2WikiMultiHopQA ensures that multi-hop reasoning is genuinely required for each question, addressing previous concerns about pseudo multi-hop questions in existing datasets.

MuSiQue is a reading comprehension dataset designed to address the prevalent issue of shortcut-based reasoning in existing multi-hop benchmarks. The dataset employs a novel bottom-up construction methodology that systematically selects and composes pairs of single-hop questions from five existing datasets, ensuring that the resulting 2 -4 hop questions require genuine connected reasoning where each step critically depends on information from previous steps. MuSiQue is offered in two variants: MuSiQue-Answerable, containing 25 K answerable questions, and MuSiQue-Full, which doubles the dataset size by pairing each answerable question with a closely similar unanswerable counterpart. The task requires models to identify answers and supporting textbfs from contexts containing up to 20 textbfs, with the additional challenge in MuSiQue-Full being first to determine answerability. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that MuSiQue presents substantial challenges compared to existing datasets, with a 3 × increase in the human-machine performance gap and a 30 -point drop in F1 score for single-hop models, confirming its effectiveness in preventing disconnected reasoning strategies and promoting genuine multi-hop comprehension.

Bamboogle is a manually curated, multi-hop question-answering dataset that addresses the limitations of template-based automatic generation found in larger datasets, such as MuSiQue, 2WikiMultiHop, and CC. Comprising 125 carefully crafted 2 -hop questions, Bamboogle is constructed through a human-centered approach where annotators read random Wikipedia articles and formulate questions by connecting two unrelated facts about the article's topic. For instance, from the Voyager 2 article, annotators might combine the facts that it was the first probe to approach Uranus and was launched on a Titan IIIE rocket to create the question 'What rocket was the first spacecraft that ever approached Uranus launched on?' A unique quality control mechanism involves filtering questions through internet search engines, including only those that fail to produce correct featured snippets, thereby ensuring the questions represent genuinely challenging, multi-hop reasoning tasks that are not readily answerable from web searches. Despite its smaller scale, Bamboogle's diverse, non-templated questions provide a valuable testbed for evaluating systems' abilities to perform authentic complex question decomposition beyond pattern matching.

## A.3. Baselines

We employ various baselines to evaluate our proposed ParallelSearch , including R1 without search engine (Guo et al., 2025), Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025b), ZeroSearch (Sun et al., 2025), StepSearch (Wang et al., 2025b), and OTC (Wang et al., 2025a).

R1 without search engine directly prompts DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B to answer the given questions without using any search engine to fetch external data sources.

Search-R1 introduces a reinforcement learning framework that trains large language models to interleave reasoning with search engine interactions autonomously. Unlike traditional retrieval-augmented generation approaches that perform single-round retrieval, Search-R1 enables LLMs to dynamically generate multiple search queries during step-by-step reasoning by learning to produce special tokens ( &lt;search&gt; and &lt;/search&gt; ) that trigger search engine calls. The framework utilizes reinforcement learning algorithms (PPO or GRPO) to optimize the LLM's ability to determine when external knowledge is required, formulate relevant search queries, and integrate retrieved information into ongoing reasoning processes. To ensure stable training, Search-R1 implements retrieved token masking, which prevents the model from optimizing over externally retrieved content, and uses simple outcome-based rewards that assess only the correctness of final answers. This

approach allows the model to learn effective search strategies through trial and error, developing capabilities for self-verification and iterative refinement without requiring explicit supervision on search behavior.

ZeroSearch presents a reinforcement learning framework that trains LLMs to develop search capabilities without requiring interaction with real search engines. The key innovation lies in using a lightweight finetuned LLM as a simulated search engine that can generate both useful and noisy documents in response to queries. During RL training, ZeroSearch employs a curriculum-based rollout strategy where the quality of generated documents is progressively degraded over time, exposing the policy model to increasingly challenging retrieval scenarios. This approach addresses two critical limitations of training with real search engines: the unpredictability of document quality and the prohibitive API costs associated with frequent search requests during RL rollouts. The framework is compatible with various RL algorithms (REINFORCE, PPO, GRPO). It demonstrates that even a 3B parameter simulation LLM can effectively incentivize search capabilities in the policy model, with larger simulators achieving performance comparable to or exceeding those of real search engines.

StepSearch introduces a reinforcement learning framework that trains LLMs as search agents through step-wise proximal policy optimization. Unlike previous search-RL methods that rely solely on global answer rewards, StepSearch augments PPO with fine-grained, token-level supervision that evaluates each search action based on information gain and redundancy penalties. The framework enforces a structured think-search-answer loop where the agent iteratively formulates search queries, retrieves documents from external sources, and incorporates the information into its reasoning process. At each search step, the method computes rewards by measuring the marginal information contribution of retrieved documents toward answering sub-questions (using cosine similarity over TF-IDF representations) while penalizing redundant retrievals that overlap with previously seen content. This step-wise supervision mechanism enables the model to decompose complex multi-hop queries into focused search subtasks and adapt its retrieval strategy dynamically.

## A.4. Implementation Details

We conduct experiments using Qwen-2.5-7B (Base/Instruct) models (Yang et al., 2024) as the backbone of the search agent, E5 (Wang et al., 2022) as the embedding model. For MultihopRAG, we use the data provided by the benchmark as the corpus; for other benchmarks, 2018 Wikipedia dump (Karpukhin et al., 2020) is utilized as the corpus. To ensure fair comparison, we follow Jin et al. (2025b) and set the number of retrieved passages to 3 across all retrieval-based methods. All experiments are conducted on 8 NVIDIA H100 GPUs.

For PPO, we set the learning rate of the policy LLM and the value LLM to 1 e -6 and 1 e -5 , respectively. The number of training steps is 500 , with warm-up ratios of 0 . 285 and 0 . 015 for the policy and value LLMs, respectively. We set 𝜆 = 1 and 𝛾 = 1 for using Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE). The total batch size is 512 , with a mini-batch size of 256 and a micro-batch size of 64 . The maximum sequence length is set to 4 , 096 tokens. The maximum response length is 500 and a maximum length is 500 tokens for retrieved content. We enable gradient checkpointing and use Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) with CPU offloading to optimize GPU memory usage. We utilize vLLM with a tensor parallel size of 1 , GPU memory utilization ratio of 0 . 6 , a temperature of 1 . 0 , a top-p value of 1 . 0 for rollout. The KL divergence regularization coefficient 𝛽 and clip ratio 𝜖 are set to 0 . 001 and 0 . 2 , respectively.
