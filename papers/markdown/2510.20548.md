## Abstract

Reinforcement learning has recently shown promise in improving retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). Despite these advances, its effectiveness in multi-hop question answering (QA) remains constrained by two fundamental issues: (i) global planning absence to structure multi-step reasoning, and (ii) unfaithful execution, which hinders effective query formulation and consistent use of retrieved evidence. We propose GlobalRAG , a reinforcement learning framework designed to enhance global reasoning in multi-hop QA. GlobalRAG decomposes questions into subgoals, coordinates retrieval with reasoning, and refines evidence iteratively. To guide this process, we introduce Planning Quality Reward and SubGoal Completion Reward , which encourage coherent planning and reliable subgoal execution. In addition, a progressive weight annealing strategy balances process-oriented and outcomebased objectives. Extensive experiments on both in-domain and out-of-domain benchmarks demonstrate that GlobalRAG significantly outperforms strong baselines while using only 8k training data (42% of the training data used by strong baselines), achieving average improvements of 14.2% in both EM and F1.

## 1 Introduction

Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) has recently demonstrated strong empirical gains (Guo et al., 2025; Dong et al., 2025; Lai et al., 2025), particularly in retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), where RL-based methods achieve competitive performance by explicitly optimizing reasoning and retrieval behaviors (Jin et al., 2025a; Chen et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025a). Despite this progress, existing approaches still struggle with multi-hop and compositional questions, where reasoning requires long-horizon planning and consistent execution across multiple retrieval steps.

Recent studies attempt to mitigate this limitation by encouraging more frequent or structured retrieval, such as StepSearch (Wang et al., 2025), LeTS (Zhang et al., 2025), ParallelSearch (Zhao et al., 2025), and GraphRAG-R1 (Yu et al., 2025). While these methods alleviate shallow retrieval to some extent, they do not fundamentally address the dominant failure modes in multi-hop QA.

Through an empirical analysis on five representative multi-hop benchmarks-HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA, MuSiQue, Bamboogle, and WikiHop-we find that the majority of errors arise from the model's inability to retrieve documents containing the correct answer after multiple retrieval steps.

A finer-grained inspection of reasoning trajectories further reveals two dominant causes: global planning absence , where the model fails to construct a coherent decomposition of the question, and unfaithful execution , where retrieval and reasoning drift away from the intended subgoals. Together, these two issues account for over 94% of failures in cases where the correct answer is missing (details in Appendix B).

Motivated by these observations, we propose GlobalRAG , a reinforcement learning framework designed to enhance global reasoning in multi-hop QA. To mitigate global planning absence, GlobalRAG introduces planning-aware optimization that explicitly decomposes questions into structured subgoals, guided by a Planning Quality Reward measuring both structural and semantic consistency. To address unfaithful execution, we further design a SubGoal Completion Reward that encourages faithful alignment between planned subgoals and executed retrieval-reasoning steps. These objectives are jointly optimized using GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), enabling stable and coherent long-horizon reasoning. Additionally, inspired by TIRESRAGR1 (He et al., 2025), we adopt a progressive weight annealing strategy to balance process-level supervision and final-answer optimization during training.

Experiments across diverse in-domain and outof-domain benchmarks demonstrate that GlobalRAG consistently outperforms strong baselines, yielding substantial gains in multi-hop reasoning accuracy.

Our contributions can be summarized as follows:

- We identify two fundamental limitations of existing RL-based approaches for multi-hop QA: global planning absence and unfaithful execution.
- We propose GlobalRAG, a reinforcement learning framework that introduces planningaware optimization to strengthen global reasoning in multi-hop QA.
- Extensive experiments on both in-domain and out-of-domain datasets demonstrate that GlobalRAG consistently outperforms strong baselines, yielding average gains of 14.2% in both EM and F1.

## 2 Related Work

Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Multi-hop QA. Multi-hop QA requires multi-step reasoning over evidence from multiple sources (Li and Du, 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2025). Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) addresses this challenge by coupling retrieval with generation (Ji et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024). Existing RAG methods for multi-hop QA mainly fall into two categories. Graph-structured approaches (Gutiérrez et al., 2025; Gutiérrez et al., 2025; Fang et al., 2025) organize textual knowledge into entity-relation graphs and aggregate evidence along multi-hop paths, but require explicit graph construction and maintenance (Gutiérrez et al., 2025; Gutiérrez et al., 2025). Iterative RAG approaches (Trivedi et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025) perform retrieval and reasoning in multiple steps (e.g., IRCOT (Trivedi et al., 2023), Search-o1 (Li et al., 2025)), yet heavily rely on LLM-generated intermediate thoughts and are thus prone to hallucination (Liu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2025). In contrast, GlobalRAG leverages reinforcement learning to enhance global planning and coordinated reasoning, enabling more accurate evidence localization and integration for multi-hop QA.

Large Language Models and Reinforcement Learning. Reinforcement learning has substantially improved complex reasoning and selfverification in LLM post-training (OpenAI et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025; Dong et al., 2025; Lai et al., 2025), and has recently been extended to RAG (Jin et al., 2025a; Chen et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025a; Wang et al., 2025). Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025a) adopts outcome-based rewards to encourage retriever usage, while StepSearch (Wang et al., 2025) scores per-step retrieval quality via information gain and redundancy penalties. However, lacking explicit global planning and suffering from unfaithful execution, these methods remain suboptimal for multi-hop QA (Jin et al., 2025a; Wang et al., 2025). By contrast, we introduce a Planning Quality Reward and a Subgoal Completion Reward to explicitly reinforce global reasoning and structured progress in multi-hop QA.

## 3.2 Global Planning-Aware Reward Design

While reinforcement learning has been shown effective for RAG, existing approaches that rely primarily on simple rule-based rewards (such as finalanswer or format rewards) tend to encourage shallow retrieval and short-horizon optimization, particularly in multi-hop QA settings where explicit global planning signals are absent. This often leads the model to deviate from the original reasoning intent during intermediate steps. To mitigate this is- sue, we propose Global Planning-Aware Rewards , which provide process-level supervision to better align long-horizon reasoning with the overall task objective. Rather than enforcing strict imitation of a single reference plan, our design adopts a soft, similarity-based alignment mechanism that assigns partial credit to diverse yet valid planning structures, which we empirically observe to vary in subgoal ordering and granularity while remaining task-consistent . This formulation promotes flexible but faithful reasoning and reduces overfitting to specific planning patterns. Concretely, our approach integrates three complementary components: Structural Consistency , Semantic Consistency , and Subgoal Completion rewards.

## 3.2.1 Planning Quality Reward

Our reward design evaluates the consistency between the rollout plan and the golden plan, both represented as directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) where nodes are subgoals and edges denote dependencies (Figure 2). We introduce two rewards: Structural Consistency Reward , measuring graph-level alignment, and Semantic Consistency Reward , assessing subgoal-level similarity. These rewards encourage more faithful and coherent reasoning.

Structural Consistency Reward enforces global planning awareness by evaluating the full dependency structure of complex tasks. In contrast to step-wise RL methods that optimize only the current step (Song et al., 2025b; He et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025), we assess the entire graph topology to capture long-range dependencies and inter-task relationships. To quantify structural differences, we employ graph edit distance-the minimum number of operations required to transform G into ˜ G -and map it into a similarity score in [0 , 1] using an exponential function, with perfect alignment yielding the maximum reward.

## 3.2.2 Subgoal Completion Reward

This reward addresses the problem of unfaithful execution by encouraging the model to follow the global plan and produce reliable outputs for each subgoal. Unlike StepSearch (Wang et al., 2025), which applies rewards directly to the token sequence of generated search queries to stabilize training and increase signal density, we argue that this strategy overly constrains exploration and discourages the discovery of novel reasoning paths. Instead, our reward design focuses solely on the correctness of subgoal answers, without penalizing the search process, thereby leaving room for more exploratory and heuristic solution strategies.

Baselines To evaluate the effectiveness of GlobalRAG, we compare against representative baselines spanning prompting- and reinforcement-learning paradigms as well as static and dynamic retrieval strategies, organized into three categories: (1) Inference without Retrieval : Zero-Shot, where the model answers solely from parametric knowledge without external context. ## 4.2 Main Results

Table 1 compares GlobalRAG with competitive RLtrained and non-RL baselines across five datasets, with the following observations:

- (1) Overall SOTA with strong gains: GlobalRAG consistently outperforms all baselines, achieving the highest F1 on all datasets and four best/one second-best EM scores. Compared with StepSearch, average F1 improves by 14.2% (HotpotQA +9.7%, 2Wiki +23.5%, MuSiQue +5.1%, Bamboogle +17.9%, WikiHop +5.6%), demonstrating the effectiveness of our planning-aware RL framework in multi-hop QA.
- (2) Gains correlate with dataset structure and multi-hop necessity: Performance improvements vary with dataset design and multi-hop requirements. WikiHop and HotpotQA include many questions solvable with single-hop evidence, where extra retrieval can add noise (Chen and Durrett, 2019). In contrast, 2Wiki's cross-document dependencies make iterative planning more effective. MuSiQue requires complex multi-hop reasoning; GlobalRAG outperforms Search-R1, though gains over StepSearch are smaller since it already excels on its training distribution. Overall, the method shows the largest advantage when multi-hop reasoning and explicit cross-document aggregation are essential.
- (3) Robust in-domain and out-of-domain performance: GlobalRAG leads on both in-domain (HotpotQA, 2Wiki, MuSiQue) and out-of-domain datasets (Bamboogle, WikiHop), indicating strong generalization. By contrast, Search-R1 performs well in-domain but degrades notably outof-domain, highlighting generalization challenges of existing methods.
- (4) Efficiency in Data, Training, and InferenceTime Trade-offs: Using only 8k examples (42% of StepSearch's 19k; 4.7% of Search-R1's 170k), GlobalRAG achieves both 14.2% EM/F1 gains over StepSearch and 28.2% EM, 29.4% F1 gains over Search-R1. ## 5 Conclusions

In this work, we presented GlobalRAG, a reinforcement learning framework for multi-hop question answering that explicitly enforces global planning and faithful execution. By modeling question decomposition as a structured plan and optimizing planning-quality and subgoal-completion rewards under a progressive annealing schedule, GlobalRAG aligns high-level reasoning intent with retrieval and execution behaviors. Extensive experiments across in-domain and out-of-domain benchmarks show that this plan-centric optimization consistently improves multi-hop reasoning accuracy without increasing retrieval cost. These results highlight the importance of supervising how reasoning unfolds, rather than optimizing final answers alone, and point to future work on more flexible planning and reasoning trajectories.

## Limitations

Our study has four main limitations. First, due to computational and cost constraints, we do not conduct reinforcement learning training on very large-scale models (e.g., 14B or 32B parameters). Whether the proposed framework can be effectively scaled to such model sizes while maintaining stable training dynamics and planning performance remains an open question. Second, our experiments focus on multi-hop question answering. We do not include a detailed analysis or dedicated training data generation for single-hop tasks, which may limit the applicability of our findings to other reasoning settings. Third, for complex multi-hop queries, multiple valid decomposition paths may exist. Although the similarity-based reward design allows partial credit for semantically aligned plans, the current training setup still optimizes toward a single reference trajectory. Explicitly modeling and optimizing over multiple valid planning paths is left for future work. Finally, the framework improves planning accuracy primarily through reinforcement learning during training, but does not incorporate an explicit mechanism for real-time error correction at inference. If the model produces an incorrect initial decomposition, it cannot revise the global plan based on intermediate retrieval failures or reasoning inconsistencies, which remains a challenge for achieving more robust and adaptive multi-hop reasoning.

## D Golden Trajectory Generation with Global Planning

To provide high-quality supervision for learning planning-aware reasoning policies, we construct golden reasoning trajectories that explicitly encode both global task decomposition and step-bystep execution. These trajectories serve as expert demonstrations for evaluating and optimizing model rollouts, ensuring alignment between highlevel planning decisions and local reasoning actions. Throughout the entire trajectory construction process, GPT-4o is used as the underlying model.

The trajectory construction process is performed offline and consists of three main stages.

## D.1 Global Planning

Given an input multi-hop question, a fixed teacher model, operating in an instruction-following mode with frozen parameters, first performs global task analysis to derive an explicit execution plan.

Specifically, the teacher model conducts a semantic analysis of the input query to identify its logical structure, constraints, and underlying reasoning type (e.g., bridge, comparison, or compositional reasoning). Based on this analysis, the original question is systematically decomposed into a sequence of fine-grained subqueries, each formulated as a natural-language question that can be independently resolved via retrieval and reasoning.

Each subquery is associated with a placeholder identifier that represents its expected intermediate result. These placeholders are subsequently referenced by downstream subqueries to explicitly capture information dependencies. The resulting subqueries and their dependency relations are organized into a structured representation, such as a task dependency graph, which defines a partial order over subgoals and serves as a global execution plan.

The entire plan is generated in a single forward pass of the teacher model using a predefined planning prompt, and remains fixed throughout subsequent stages, ensuring consistency and reproducibility.

## D.2 Subgoal Execution

Given the global plan, the teacher model resolves each subtask in topological order according to the dependency structure.

For each subquery, the model performs localized reasoning, including evidence retrieval, information synthesis, and intermediate answer generation. Retrieval is conducted using the same dense retriever employed during training and inference, without access to oracle documents, to ensure that the generated trajectories reflect realistic reasoning conditions.

Once a subtask is completed, its generated answer is substituted into the corresponding placeholder and propagated to all dependent subqueries. This explicit result propagation mechanism ensures that later reasoning steps operate on an updated context that incorporates previously resolved information, thereby maintaining global coherence.

Throughout this process, retrieval queries are conditioned on both the original question and the current intermediate context, enabling targeted evidence acquisition for each subgoal. The execution of each subtask is recorded as a sequence of stateaction-result tuples, forming a fine-grained trace of the reasoning process.

## D.3 Final Answer Generation

After all subtasks have been resolved, the intermediate answers are integrated according to the dependency graph to produce a globally consistent final answer.

Additionally, to ensure the reliability and internal consistency of the constructed golden trajectories, we apply a set of strict filtering criteria throughout the generation process. First, at the global planning stage, if the execution plan produced by the teacher model violates the predefined topological constraints of the task dependency graph-e.g., introducing cyclic dependencies or inconsistent subgoal ordering-the corresponding sample is discarded. Second, during subgoal execution, each subquery is allowed up to 15 retrieval attempts; if the teacher model fails to derive a valid intermediate answer within this budget, the entire trajectory is removed to avoid propagating incomplete or unstable reasoning traces. Finally, after final answer generation, trajectories whose predicted answers do not exactly match the ground-truth annotations are excluded. Together, these filtering rules prioritize structural correctness, execution feasibility, and answer-level accuracy, resulting in a high-quality set of expert demonstrations suitable for training and evaluation. Detailed prompt we use can be found in PER-PSE (Liu et al., 2025).

## G Quantitative Analysis of Plan Decomposition Diversity

To further examine whether GlobalRAG relies on a single predefined reasoning trajectory or is able to generate diverse plan decompositions, we conduct a quantitative analysis on the diversity of planning behaviors at inference time. This study is performed on the BAMBOOGLE dataset using a QWEN2.5-3B-INSTRUCT model fine-tuned under the GlobalRAG framework.

For each question, we sample five independent responses with identical decoding configurations. In total, this results in 625 generated answers for 125 questions. Among these generations, 254 answers are judged to be correct according to the dataset evaluation protocol. To assess plan-level diversity, we further group correct answers by their corresponding plan decompositions, where a plan is defined as the sequence of intermediate reasoning steps and sub-question structures produced by the model. Duplicate plans for the same question are collapsed into a single instance.

After deduplication, we obtain 144 distinct correct plan decompositions. This indicates that a substantial portion of correct answers arises from multiple, non-identical reasoning plans rather than repeated execution of a single trajectory. Quantitatively, over 56% of correct generations correspond to distinct plan decompositions, suggesting that the learned policy is capable of exploring alternative reasoning structures that still lead to valid answers.

These results provide empirical evidence that GlobalRAG is not strictly constrained to reproducing a single golden plan during inference. Instead, the model demonstrates a degree of flexibility in plan construction, generating multiple viable decompositions for the same question. While repeated plans remain common-reflecting stable high-reward reasoning patterns-the presence of a large number of distinct correct plans indicates that the planning policy learned by GlobalRAG is not limited to a fixed, deterministic trajectory.

We emphasize that this analysis is intended to establish the existence of plan-level diversity rather than to claim optimal or exhaustive exploration of the plan space. Nonetheless, the observed diversity supports the view that GlobalRAG can move beyond rigid adherence to predefined reasoning paths and instead learn a more flexible planning behavior under reinforcement learning.

Table 9 reports the performance of GlobalRAG trained with GRPO under different group size (n) settings across multiple multi-hop question answering benchmarks. As n increases from 1 to 5, we observe consistent improvements in both EM and F1 on all datasets.

From an algorithmic perspective, this trend can be attributed to the statistical properties of advantage estimation in GRPO. Unlike policy optimization methods that rely on an explicit value function,

GRPO estimates advantages using relative rewards within a sampled group, where the group-wise average reward serves as an implicit baseline. When the group size is small, this baseline estimate is more susceptible to noise from individual samples, resulting in higher variance in the advantage signal and less stable policy updates. Increasing the group size yields a more reliable estimate of the expected reward under the current policy, thereby providing a more accurate reference point for computing relative advantages.

This effect is particularly pronounced on reasoning-intensive benchmarks such as HotpotQA, 2Wiki, and WikiHop. For instance, on HotpotQA, increasing n from 1 to 5 leads to gains of 5.7 EM and 6.2 F1 points, indicating that a more stable relative advantage signal helps the model better discriminate among retrieval-reasoning trajectories of varying quality. On smaller or noisier datasets such as Musique and Bamboogle, the improvements are more modest but remain consistent, suggesting that larger group sizes primarily contribute to variance reduction during training rather than exploiting dataset-specific artifacts.

At the same time, the gains exhibit diminishing returns as the group size increases, highlighting a trade-off between the accuracy of baseline estimation and computational cost. Based on this observation, we adopt n=5 as the default group size in the remaining experiments unless otherwise specified.
