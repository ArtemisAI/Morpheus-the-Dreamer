## Abstract

Recent search-augmented LLMs trained with reinforcement learning (RL) can interleave searching and reasoning for multi-hop reasoning tasks. However, they face two critical failure modes as the accumulating context becomes flooded with both crucial evidence and irrelevant information: (1) ineffective search chain construction that produces incorrect queries or omits retrieval of critical information, and (2) reasoning hijacking by peripheral evidence that causes models to misidentify distractors as valid evidence. To address these challenges, we propose D 2 PLAN, a D ual-agent D ynamic global Plan ning paradigm for complex retrieval-augmented reasoning. D 2 PLAN operates through the collaboration of a Reasoner and a Purifier : the Reasoner constructs explicit global plans during reasoning and dynamically adapts them based on retrieval feedback; the Purifier assesses retrieval relevance and condenses key information for the Reasoner . We further introduce a two-stage training framework consisting of supervised finetuning (SFT) cold-start on synthesized trajectories and RL with plan-oriented rewards to teach LLMs to master the D 2 PLAN paradigm. Extensive experiments demonstrate that D 2 PLAN enables more coherent multi-step reasoning and stronger resilience to irrelevant information, thereby achieving superior performance on challenging QA benchmarks.

## 1 Introduction

Recent advancements in reinforcement learning (RL) have empowered Large Language Models (LLMs) with the capability to perform autonomous retrieval during reasoning (Song et al., 2025; Jin et al., 2025b; Chen et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025; Zheng et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). Transcending the traditional Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) paradigm (Lewis et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2023), these models are no longer passive consumers of retrieved documents. Instead, they

actively determine when , what , and how to search, subsequently integrating the retrieved information into their reasoning processes (Lin et al., 2025), thereby enhancing performance on knowledgeintensive multi-hop reasoning tasks.

However, we observe that as LLMs iteratively invoke retrievers to gather evidence for complex questions, the context becomes inundated with documents retrieved at various steps. This lengthy context, containing both key evidence and substantial irrelevant information, dilutes the model's attention to the cues in the original question and retrieved critical evidence (Hsieh et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2025). As shown in Figure 1 (Left), the model loses track of temporal constraints in the original question, leading to a newly constructed query that lacks global context and causes a drift in retrieval targets. In other scenarios (Figure 1, Right), when faced with a lengthy context flooded with all retrieved information, the model struggles to discriminate between valid evidence and distractors, leading to erroneous conclusions derived from irrelevant information. We categorize these pervasive failure modes into two primary types: (E1) Ineffective Search Chain Construction: The query sequences iteratively generated by models often contain incorrect queries, causing divergence from the original reasoning objective, or lack necessary queries, omitting retrieval of essential knowledge

points. (E2) Reasoning Hijacking by Peripheral Evidence: Models tend to misidentify irrelevant information in retrieved results as valid evidence, and fail to re-retrieve when retrieval yields entirely irrelevant results.

After analyzing the results of state-of-the-art methods, we find that both E1 and E2 are prevalent and account for 29% and 63% of total failures, respectively (§4.6). These phenomena suggest that to better deal with complex multi-hop reasoning tasks, models need precise perception and control over the retrieval-augmented reasoning process. Specifically, two core capabilities are essential: (C1) Dynamic Global Planning: The model must be capable of formulating an explicit global plan and maintaining awareness of it throughout the reasoning process. Furthermore, it should be able to dynamically adapt this plan based on retrieval feedback, including revising sub-questions and rewriting queries. This ability prevents the drift in retrieval targets and the omission of critical reasoning or retrieval steps, while enabling recovery from retrieval failures. (C2) Robust Resistance to Peripheral Evidence: The model should be able accurately distinguish key evidence from irrelevant information in retrieved results. This capability ensures reasoning is grounded in reliable evidence and enables the model to recognize retrieval failures, thereby performing self-correction by triggering plan adaptation in C1.

To equip models with such capabilities, we propose D 2 PLAN, a D ual-agent D ynamic global Plan ning paradigm supported by a two-stage training framework. D 2 PLAN operates through the collaboration of two agents: a Reasoner and a Purifier . The Reasoner performs retrieval-augmented reasoning with explicit dynamic global planning, while the Purifier evaluates retrieval relevance and distills key information for the Reasoner . For a given question, the Reasoner autonomously constructs a global plan (i.e., an ordered sequence of sub-questions), sequentially addresses each subquestion, and dynamically adapts the plan based on feedback from the Purifier .

The training process of D 2 PLAN consists of two phases: SFT Cold-Start and SPLANRL. In the first phase, we design a pipeline to manually implement the workflow of D 2 PLAN and synthesize data using a powerful teacher model to fine-tune both the Reasoner and Purifier . This cold-start stage enables the Reasoner to acquire preliminary reasoning capabilities with dynamic global planning, and enables the Purifier to learn accurate relevance assessment and information condensation. In the second phase, we introduce SPLANRL, which utilizes plan-oriented rewards to enhance the Reasoner 's reasoning capability by guiding it to correctly formulate and adapt global plans. To validate the effectiveness of D 2 PLAN, we conducted extensive experiments on challenging QA benchmarks. Notably, our method achieves an average absolute performance improvement of 3.8% over the stateof-the-art baseline across six datasets.

Our contributions are as follows: (1) We identify two prevalent failure modes of existing LLMs when handling lengthy retrieval-augmented reasoning contexts: Ineffective Search Chain Construction and Reasoning Hijacking by Peripheral Evidence. (2) We propose D 2 PLAN, a dual-agent dynamic planning paradigm supported by a twostage training framework, to enhance the global planning and resistance to peripheral evidence of search-augmented LLMs, thereby mitigating these failure modes. (3) Extensive experiments demonstrate that D 2 PLAN enables more coherent query formulation and stronger resilience to peripheral evidence during reasoning, achieving superior performance on challenging QA benchmarks.

## 2 Methodology

In this section, we present our D 2 PLAN paradigm and the corresponding training framework. First, we provide an overview of the D 2 PLAN workflow 1 , introducing how its dual-agent architecture and dynamic global planning mechanism operate when solving challenging problems (§2.1). Then, we detail how we teach LLMs to master this paradigm through a two-stage training framework comprising SFT cold-start (§2.2) and SPLANRL (§2.3).

## 2.1 Overview of D 2 PLAN

Dual-Agent Architecture As shown in Figure 3, D 2 PLAN comprises two agents: a Reasoner and a Purifier . The Reasoner is responsible for executing core planning, reasoning, and invoking search tools. When invoking search tools, the Reasoner generates a query and specifies the target sub-question to be solved. A retriever returns candidate documents based on the query. These documents are not directly passed to the Reasoner , but first analyzed by the Purifier . The Purifier focuses on assessing whether the candidate documents contain key in-

The purified information and relevance judgment are then fed back to the Reasoner , enabling it to adapt the global plan and continue reasoning.

Dynamic Global Planning As shown in Figure 2, given an input question, the Reasoner autonomously assesses its complexity and constructs a global problem-solving plan. Single-hop questions require no decomposition, whereas multi-hop questions are decomposed into an ordered sequence of sub-questions to form an initial plan. In this sequence, each sub-question depends only on preceding ones and can reference their answers using placeholders like #A\_j (denoting the answer to the j -th sub-question), which allows the Reasoner to solve them sequentially. When solving each sub-question, the Reasoner can iteratively generate queries and invoke search tools based on feed- back from the Purifier , continuing until relevant evidence is obtained or the maximum number of attempts K is reached. Upon successfully solving a sub-question, the Reasoner refines the subsequent sub-question based on the resolved context, making it more specific and self-contained to facilitate effective retrieval and prevent divergence from the original reasoning objective. Conversely, if a subquestion remains unsolved after reaching the maximum retrieval attempts, the Reasoner revises the remaining sub-questions, including the current unsolved one, to explore alternative problem-solving paths for self-correction.

## 2.2 SFT Cold-Start

We use SFT to train the dual agents, guiding Reasoner to acquire preliminary retrieval-augmented reasoning capabilities with dynamic global planning, and enabling Purifier to master relevance assessment and information condensation.

## 2.2.1 Synthesizing Trajectories

To obtain high-quality SFT training data, we design a pipeline that guides a powerful teacher LLM to play the roles of Reasoner and Purifier . 2 Specifically, we employ a series of curated prompts (see Appendix J) to step-by-step guide the teacher model to simulate the workflow described in §2.1, executing a series of operations including question

Each trajectory is also accompanied by the corresponding inputoutput pairs of the Purifier for relevance judgment and information purification at each retrieval step.

## 2.2.2 Constructing SFT Data

To construct high-quality SFT training data, we first filter the synthesized reasoning trajectories based on two criteria: (1) the final answer exactly matches the gold answer; (2) the trajectory contains at least one retrieval call. The second criterion ensures that the model prioritizes learning to leverage retrieval tools for problem-solving rather than relying on potentially erroneous internal parametric knowledge. Based on this filtered set of high-quality trajectories, we construct corresponding training data for both agents. For the Reasoner , we further use the teacher LLM to reformat the filtered reasoning trajectories, integrating the segmented reasoning steps synthesized via multiple prompts into a coherent and fluent reasoning chain that serves as the training target. For the Purifier , we extract the input-output pairs corresponding to each retrieval step from the filtered reasoning trajectories as its training data.

## 2.3 SPLANRL

After the SFT stage, we freeze the Purifier and introduce SPLANRL to further enhance the Reasoner 's reasoning capability by strengthening its dynamic planning proficiency. In addition to the commonly used format and answer rewards in general RL, we design plan-oriented rewards to effectively guide the model's dynamic planning behavior, incentivizing both the quality of plan construction and the effectiveness of plan adjustments.

Initial Planning Reward This reward incentivizes the model to identify the required number of reasoning steps and construct high-quality initial plans. Specifically, for single-hop questions, we assign R p = 1 if the model does not perform question decomposition, and R p = 0 otherwise. For multihop questions, we assign R p = 1 if the model performs question decomposition and the number of generated sub-questions matches the hop count annotated in the dataset, and R p = 0 otherwise. We use count-based rather than semantic constraints for two reasons. First, count-based constraints do not require annotations of sub-questions and sub- answers, making them more practical to implement. Second, count-based constraints effectively prevent the model from generating redundant sub-questions or missing necessary ones, thereby improving the conciseness and effectiveness of planning (§4.4).

Plan Adaptation Reward This reward aims to reinforce the model's capability of dynamically adjusting plans throughout the reasoning process, encompassing two complementary mechanisms: plan refinement and plan revision .

Plan Refinement Reward. For each sub-question that depends on the preceding ones, we use the Plan Refinement Reward R refine to encourage the model to refine it after solving those dependenciesfilling in placeholders and making subsequent subquestions more specific and self-contained. A successful refinement requires that the refined subquestion exists in the reasoning trajectory and contains no placeholders, which we verify using regular expression matching. If all such sub-questions are successfully refined, the reward R refine = 1 , and R refine = 0 otherwise. This reward not only encourages the model to improve sub-question quality, but also effectively prevents the drift in retrieval targets during multi-hop reasoning.

Plan Revision Reward. We design a revision reward to encourage the model to globally revise the remaining sub-questions when iterative retrieval reaches the maximum number of attempts without finding relevant information. Such failures are often caused by poorly formulated sub-questions, such as those that are overly vague or lack necessary constraints. This reward is jointly determined by timing reward R t and quality reward R q . R t = 1 if the model triggers revision only after retrieval reaches the maximum number of failures, preventing premature abandonment; otherwise R t = 0 . R q = 1 if the first revised sub-question (i.e., the current unsolved sub-question after revision) successfully retrieves relevant information; otherwise R q = 0 . We use the relevance judgment from the Purifier to determine whether the retrieval is successful. Combining the two terms, our Plan Adaptation Reward is defined as R a = R refine + R revise .

Answer Reward Following recent work (Zheng et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025), we adopt the wordlevel F1 score between the predicted answer and the ground truth to measure answer correctness. The answer reward is defined as R ans = F1 ( a pred , a gt ) , where F1 ( a pred , a gt ) denotes the F1 score between the predicted answer a pred and the golden answer a gt .

Format Reward We treat the format reward as a prerequisite constraint, aiming to ensure the model to generate parsable reasoning paths, which is essential for computing the Initial Planning Reward R p and Plan Adaptation Reward R a . For example, the model must wrap the initial plan with &lt;plan&gt;&lt;/plan&gt; tags, the revised plan with &lt;replan&gt;&lt;/replan&gt; tags, and the final answer with &lt;answer&gt;&lt;/answer&gt; tags (see Appendix F for detailed constraints). When all constraints are satisfied, R f = 1 , otherwise 0.

Total Reward The final total reward function is defined as: R total = R f · ( αR p + βR a + R ans ) , where α and β are scaling factors that control the contribution of each reward component.

## 3 Experimental Setup

Datasets and Evaluation Metrics To comprehensively evaluate the capabilities of D 2 PLAN, we conduct experiments on widely used open-domain QA benchmarks, including Natural Questions (NQ) (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), SimpleQA (Wei et al., 2024), HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), 2WikiMultihopQA (2Wiki) (Ho et al., 2020), MuSiQue (MSQ) (Trivedi et al., 2022b), and FRAMES (Krishna et al., 2025). For evaluation metrics, we employ Exact Match (EM) and LLM-as-a-Judge (LasJ). For LasJ, we utilize qwen-plus (Qwen et al., 2025) to assess the correctness of predicted answers (see Appendix J for the prompt).

Baselines We compare D 2 PLAN with representative RL-based methods: Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025b), ReSearch (Chen et al., 2025), AutoRefine (Shi et al., 2025), ZeroSearch (Sun et al., 2025), R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025), and StepSearch (Zheng et al., 2025). We reproduce the inference results of all baseline methods under the same environment to ensure fair comparison.

Implementation Details We employ Qwen2.53B/7B-Instruct (Qwen et al., 2025) as the backbone models. For retrieval, we utilize E5 (Wang et al., 2022) as the embedding model and the 2018 Wikipedia dump (Karpukhin et al., 2020) as the retrieval corpus, retrieving the top-5 passages. For SFT cold-start, we use qwen-max-latest as the teacher model to synthesize 4,000 training instances for the Reasoner and 11,255 for the Purifier from the training sets of NQ, HotpotQA, and MuSiQue. For RL training, we sample 29,762 instances from the same training sets and adopt GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) as the learning algorithm, implemented on verl (Sheng et al., 2025). More implementation details are provided in Appendix C.

As shown in Table 1, D 2 PLAN consistently outperforms all baselines at both 3B and 7B scales , demonstrating strong performance and generalization across in-domain and out-of-domain benchmarks. ## 4.5 Effect of Dual-Agent Architecture (RQ4)

To validate the effectiveness of the dual-agent architecture, we train a Reasoner baseline on trajectories synthesized from the same question set, but with raw retrieval results instead of Purifier-refined outputs. This ensures the Reasoner is not biased toward refined inputs, enabling a fair comparison. We then evaluate the impact of adding the Purifier (trained in §2.2) on the FRAMES dataset.

Greater Performance Gains on Harder Questions. As shown in Table 6, incorporating the Purifier significantly improves performance across different hop counts while substantially reducing context length. The advantage of the Purifier is particularly pronounced on questions with 5 or more hops, where LasJ improves by 7.61% and context length decreases by 65.5%. We attribute the substantially worse performance of w/o Purifier to the accumulation of redundant information in raw retrieval results as question difficulty increases, which distracts the Reasoner from focusing on critical information both within the current retrieval results and from prior reasoning steps. The Purifier effectively mitigates this issue by refining retrieval results and assessing their relevance.

## 5 Related Work

## Planning in Retrieval-Augmented Generation.

Planning is crucial for RAG systems to handle complex multi-hop questions. Iterative RAG methods (Shao et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2022a; Liu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025) interleave reasoning and retrieval but typically perform local planning, leading to intent drift in lengthy histories. PlanRAG (Lee et al., 2024) introduces a structured approach that generates plans and adjusts them via iterative re-planning. However, it relies on zero-shot prompting of models without parameter optimization.

Reinforcement Learning for Search-Augmented LLMs. Recently, RL has become the dominant paradigm for training search-augmented LLMs (Jin et al., 2025b; Song et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025), as it encourages autonomous exploration and better generalization than SFT (Chu et al., 2025). Further research introduces step-wise process supervision (Zheng et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025; Luo et al., 2025a), reward density optimization (Luo et al., 2025b), and selfcorrection mechanisms (Wang et al., 2025) to enhance fine-grained reasoning and retrieval capabilities. Despite these advances, current methods struggle with noisy and lengthy retrieval-augmented reasoning contexts. Our method addresses these challenges by introducing a dual-agent dynamic global planning paradigm, optimized via a two-stage training framework.

## 6 Conclusion

We identify two prevalent failure modes of searchaugmented LLMs in complex retrieval-augmented reasoning: Ineffective Search Chain Construction and Reasoning Hijacking by Peripheral Evidence. To address these issues, we introduce D 2 PLAN, a dual-agent dynamic planning paradigm where the Reasoner performs reasoning with explicit dynamic planning and the Purifier evaluates retrieval relevance and distills key information. A twostage training framework comprising SFT coldstart and planning-oriented RL equips LLMs with the D 2 PLAN paradigm. Extensive experiments demonstrate that D 2 PLAN enables more coher-

ent query formulation and stronger resilience to peripheral evidence, achieving superior retrievalaugmented reasoning abilities.

## 7 Limitations

While D 2 PLAN demonstrates strong performance on open-domain QA benchmarks, several limitations remain. First, although we achieve significant improvements on Qwen2.5-3B and Qwen2.5-7B models, due to computational cost constraints, we are unable to validate our method on larger language models. Second, due to cost limitations, we employ a local dense retriever (E5) with a static Wikipedia corpus rather than more powerful retrieval systems such as commercial search engines (e.g., Google). As revealed in our error analysis, a notable portion of failures (E2.2) may stem from retriever or corpus limitations. Integrating stronger retrieval infrastructure could potentially further enhance the performance of D 2 PLAN. Third, our approach relies on a powerful teacher model to generate high-quality reasoning trajectories for supervised fine-tuning. This dependency may limit the applicability of our method in scenarios where access to such teacher models is restricted or costly.

## A D 2 PLAN Workflow

The inference workflow of D 2 PLAN is formalized in Algorithm 1. Given an input question Q , the Reasoner fi rst assesses its complexity. For single-hop questions, it directly performs iterative retrieval and generates the answer. For multi-hop questions, it decomposes the question into an ordered sequence of sub-questions and solves them sequentially with dynamic plan adaptation.

## B Trajectory Synthesis Strategy

Given a question Q , the trajectory synthesis process is formalized in Algorithms 3, 4, and 5.

Iterative Retrieval Loop (Algorithm 3) This component serves as the fundamental building block for retrieving information. It executes a loop for up to K attempts. In each iteration k :

1. Query Generation : The teacher model M generates a reasoning thought r k and a search query q k based on the current history H . When the initial query fails to retrieve relevant information, we employ the query rewriting strategy (Table 16) to refine the search query.
2. Retrieval and Purification : The retriever fetches documents D k , and the Purifier (acted
3. by the teacher model, Table 17) extracts information I k with a relevance judgment J k .
3. Termination : If J k is Relevant , the loop terminates successfully returning the history H . If the loop exhausts K attempts without success, it returns Failure .

Single-hop Question Processing (Algorithm 4) The process begins by assessing the question complexity. We denote the reasoning process for this assessment as r type . For single-hop questions, it directly invokes the Iterative Retrieval Loop.

- If the loop succeeds, the teacher model generates the final reasoning r final and answer a based on the collected history (Table 18). The final trajectory includes the complexity assessment, retrieval history, and the final answer.
- If the loop fails, the synthesis process is terminated.

Multi-hop Question Processing (Algorithm 5) The process begins by assessing the question complexity. We denote the reasoning process for this assessment as r type . For multi-hop questions, the process follows the dynamic global planning paradigm:

1. Initialization : The model decomposes Q into an initial sub-question plan P (Table 15).
2. Execution Loop : The model iterates through the sub-questions in P . For each sub-question q i :
- Before retrieval, we first judge whether external retrieval is necessary (Table 20). If so, it invokes the Iterative Retrieval Loop.
- Success (Plan Refinement) : If retrieval succeeds, the evidence is added to the global context C . The model answers the sub-question (Table 18) and then refines the next sub-question based on C (Table 19), making it more specific and self-contained.
- Failure (Plan Revision) : If retrieval fails, the model checks if the maximum revision count M has been reached. If not, it triggers plan revision (Table 21) to revise the remaining sub-questions based on the current context and failure information. The execution resumes with the revised plan.

## E Ablation Study Implementation Details

For the removal of the Dynamic Global Planning Paradigm ( w/o Plan&amp;Adapt ), we do not use the reasoning trajectory synthesis method described in §2.2. Instead, while retaining the dual-agent architecture, we employ the prompt provided in Table 24 to guide the teacher model in rolling out solutions. We then apply rejection sampling to retain only those reasoning trajectories that yield correct answers, which are subsequently used as SFT data. In the RL phase, we optimize using only format and answer rewards, omitting all planningrelated reward designs.

For w/o Adapt , we disable the plan adaptation mechanism, meaning the model generates an initial plan but lacks the ability to replan or perform iterative retrieval when retrieval results are irrelevant. During the RL phase, we use the format reward, answer reward, and the Initial Planning Reward R p .

We conduct SFT training using reasoning trajectories synthesized from the same question set.

For w/o R p and w/o R a , we remove the initial planning reward and adaptation reward respectively, while keeping all other components unchanged.

## F Format Reward Constraints

The format reward R f enforces the following constraints to ensure parsable reasoning paths:

1. Answer Tag Constraint : The model output must contain exactly one pair of &lt;answer&gt; and &lt;/answer&gt; tags, enclosing the final answer.
2. Plan Tag Constraint : For multi-hop questions, the output must contain &lt;plan&gt; and &lt;/plan&gt; tags that enclose the initial decomposition of sub-questions.
3. Sub-question Identifier Constraint : Within the plan, each sub-question must be marked with identifiers " #Q\_i: " (where i is the subquestion index), enabling systematic tracking of the solving process.
4. Refinement Tag Constraint : When refining a sub-question after solving its dependencies, the model must use &lt;Updated\_#Q\_i&gt; and &lt;/Updated\_#Q\_i&gt; tags to mark the refined version.

## G Comparison of Different Purifier Agents

This section extends the analysis in §4 by comparing different Purifier configurations. We use the same experimental setup described in the main text.

As shown in Table 8, we compare three categories of Purifier configurations: (1) no Purifier baseline, (2) general-purpose instruction-tuned models (Qwen2.5-7B/32B-Instruct and QwenMax), and (3) our task-specific fine-tuned models (Qwen2.5-3B/7B-SFT). Search-R1 serves as an external baseline representing the single-agent paradigm.
