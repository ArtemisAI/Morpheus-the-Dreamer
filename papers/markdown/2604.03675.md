## Abstract

In agentic search, large language models (LLMs) are trained to perform multi-turn retrieval and reasoning for complex tasks such as multi-hop question answering (QA). However, current search-based Reinforcement Learning (RL) methods suffer from two core limitations: expensive long-horizon rollouts are under-utilized during training, and supervision is typically available only at the final answer, resulting in severe reward sparsity. We present P refix-based R ollout reuse for A gentic search with I ntermediate S tep r E wards (PRAISE), a framework for improving both data efficiency and credit assignment in agentic search training. Given a complete search trajectory, PRAISE extracts prefix states at different search turns, elicits intermediate answers from them, and uses these prefixes both to construct additional training trajectories and to derive step-level rewards from performance differences across prefixes. Our method uses a single shared model for both search policy learning and prefix answer evaluation, enabling joint optimization without extra human annotations or a separate reward model. Experiments on multi-hop QA benchmarks show that PRAISE consistently improves performance over strong baselines.

## 1 Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly moving beyond one-shot generation toward agentic problem solving, where a model interacts with external tools and environments over multiple steps to reason, plan, and act (Yao et al., 2022; Schick et al., 2023). Among these emerging paradigms, agentic search has become a particularly important framework for knowledge-intensive tasks such as multi-hop question answering (QA), deep research, and open-domain reasoning (Li et al., 2025; Jin et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025). on a fixed retrieval stage as in standard retrievalaugmented generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020), agentic search enables a model to dynamically decide when to search, what to search for, and how to update its reasoning based on newly acquired evidence. This adaptive interaction with external information is crucial for solving problems whose answers cannot be obtained from parametric memory alone.

To improve search and reasoning in such systems, recent work increasingly applies reinforcement learning (RL) to agentic search. In this setting, the model executes a multi-turn search trajectory and is trained based on the quality of its final answer or overall task performance. A particularly important paradigm is reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) (Lambert et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025), where supervision comes from automatically checkable signals. By directly optimizing downstream task success, RLVR provides a natural training framework for agentic search. However, current search-based RL methods still face important limitations.

A central issue is that supervision in searchbased RL is typically available only at the trajectory level. A single rollout often contains multiple rounds of reasoning, querying, reading, and search-state updates, making it substantially more costly than ordinary single-pass generation. Yet, in most existing approaches, the full trajectory is still treated as a single training instance, with learning signals derived primarily from the final answer. This leads to poor data efficiency (Liu et al., 2025; Huang and Wan): although a rollout contains multiple informative intermediate search states, existing methods typically consume it as a single training instance rather than converting those intermediate states into additional learnable examples, limiting the value extracted from each expensive rollout. At the same time, it also results in reward sparsity : because supervision arrives only at the end of the

trajectory, it becomes difficult to determine which intermediate search actions improved answer quality and which were redundant or harmful (Zheng et al., 2025; Cui et al., 2025). This combination of under-utilized intermediate information and delayed feedback makes long-horizon credit assignment especially challenging.

Prior work has attempted to address these issues from two largely separate directions. One line of research improves sample efficiency through replay buffers (Sun et al., 2025), trajectory reuse (Wang et al., 2025b), or off-policy updates (Chen et al., 2025a). Another line introduces denser supervision via process rewards (Cui et al., 2025), teacherbased evaluation (Xie et al., 2026), or intermediate annotations (Zhang et al., 2025; Zheng et al., 2025). However, these approaches remain limited in the agentic search setting. Existing reuse methods are typically centered around whole trajectories rather than the inherently compositional structure of multi-turn search. Meanwhile, processsupervision methods often rely on additional ingredients such as gold sub-questions, annotated evidence, separately trained reward models, or frozen teacher models, all of which introduce extra cost and may create mismatch between the evaluator and the evolving search policy.

In this paper, we ask a simple but important question: how can we maximize the training value of each expensive search rollout? To answer this question, we propose PRAISE , short for P refix-based R ollout reuse for A gentic search with I ntermediate S tep r E wards. The key idea is to exploit the natural prefix structure of search trajectories. Given a complete multi-turn rollout, we extract prefix states at different search turns and prompt the model to produce intermediate answers conditioned only on the information available up to each prefix. These prefix-answer pairs are then used in two complementary ways.

First, they provide rollout reuse . PRAISE converts a full rollout into multiple prefix-based training samples. In this way, intermediate search states that would otherwise be ignored are transformed into additional supervision, substantially increasing the amount of learnable signal extracted from each rollout. Second, they provide intermediate step rewards . By evaluating the quality of answers produced from different prefixes and comparing performance across adjacent prefixes, we estimate the marginal contribution of each search step. This converts sparse terminal supervision into fine-grained process rewards aligned with the search trajectory itself. Such reward densification leads to more informative training signals and improves long-horizon credit assignment.

A central design choice of PRAISE is that the same model is used both for search policy learning and for prefix answer evaluation. This sharedmodel formulation avoids the need for an additional reward model, thereby reducing system complexity and annotation cost. More importantly, it allows search policy improvement and prefix evaluation capability to co-evolve through joint optimization, which in turn improves the consistency between the acting policy and the source of intermediate supervision (Chen et al., 2026b,a).

We evaluate PRAISE on multi-hop QA benchmarks. Experimental results show that our method consistently outperforms strong search-based RL baselines. Further analysis shows that the method is robust to key hyperparameters and that joint optimization with a shared model performs substantially better than alternatives based on frozen evaluators.

Our contributions are summarized as follows:

- We propose a prefix-based rollout reuse framework, transforming each expensive multi-turn search trajectory into multiple learnable prefix samples and improves rollout utilization.
- We introduce a process reward construction method based on adjacent prefix gains to convert sparse terminal signals into fine-grained, step-aligned rewards.
- We develop a joint optimization framework in which a single shared model performs both search decision making and prefix evaluation.
- We demonstrate the effectiveness of PRAISE on multi-hop QA benchmarks, showing consistent improvements over strong baselines.

## 2 Related Work

Agentic Search and RL-based Training. Recent advancements have focused on training LLMs as agentic search systems that interleave multi-step reasoning with external retrieval. Leveraging RL frameworks such as PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) or GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), representative agentic search methods such as Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) and R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025) have

demonstrated that such RL-based paradigms significantly bolster performance in complex search tasks. Parallel to these end-to-end agents, extensive research has investigated the optimization of specific search components, including planning (Chen et al., 2025c), query reformulation (Ma et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2025b), document selection (Ke et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a), and answer generation (Yang et al., 2025). More recently, there is a growing emphasis on the joint optimization of these modular components to achieve superior global performance (Chen et al., 2026b,a).

Data Efficiency and Rollout Reuse. Improving data efficiency remains a critical challenge in RL training for LLMs. One common strategy is data selection, for example by prioritizing questions of moderate difficulty for the current policy, which has been shown to be especially useful for GRPO-style training (Yu et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025). Another strategy is trajectory reuse, where recent rollouts are reused across multiple optimization steps through replay buffers or relaxed onpolicy updates (Sun et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025a; Wang et al., 2025b). More recently, several works study prefix reuse to reduce redundant generation. PROS (Huang and Wan) reuses valuable intermediate prefixes to form augmented training inputs, while SPEC-RL (Liu et al., 2025) treats previous rollouts as speculative drafts and resumes generation from the first unverifiable position. However, these methods are developed mainly for math or code reasoning, where 'prefixes' are defined over token sequences rather than explicit search turns.

## Process Supervision and Intermediate Rewards.

To address the reward sparsity in long-horizon reasoning, recent research has introduced denser supervision signals (Lightman et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2025a). StepSearch (Zheng et al., 2025) constructs step-wise rewards using reference subquestions, keywords, and documents, while ReasonRAG (Zhang et al., 2025) estimates intermediate utility by rolling out from partial states and favoring shorter successful reasoning paths. TIPS (Xie et al., 2026) and IGPO (Wang et al., 2025a) define turn-level rewards through changes in answer likelihood under a teacher model or the policy model itself. More broadly, PRIME (Cui et al., 2025) shows that dense process rewards can be learned online from outcome supervision alone, though its main focus is mathematical and coding reasoning. Unlike these, our PRAISE derives re- wards directly from performance gains between adjacent search prefixes, eliminating the need for gold-standard annotations or external evaluators.

## 4 Experiments

We conduct experiments to answer the following research questions:

RQ1: Does PRAISE outperform existing baselines?

RQ2: How effective is joint optimization with a shared prefix evaluator?

RQ3: What is the contribution of process rewards to the overall performance of PRAISE?

RQ4: Is PRAISE robust to different choices of the process-reward weight α , and what value works best?

## 4.1 Experimental Settings

Datasets. We train PRAISE on HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) and 2WikiMultihopQA (Ho et al., 2020), and evaluate on NQ (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), HotpotQA, 2WikiMultihopQA, Bam- boogle (Press et al., 2022), and MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022).

Baselines. We compare against four categories of baselines: (1) non-agentic methods, including direct LLM answering and naive RAG (Lewis et al., 2020); (2) agentic search methods, including Search-o1 (Li et al., 2025), Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025), and R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025); (3) process-supervision methods, including StepSearch (Zheng et al., 2025), ReasonRAG (Zhang et al., 2025), and TIPS (Xie et al., 2026).

Implementation Details. We use Qwen2.57B (Team, 2024) as the base model, verl 1 as the training framework, E5 (Wang et al., 2022) as the retriever, and Wikipedia as the retrieval corpus. We report EM and F1 as the evaluation metrics. The process-reward weight α is set to 0 . 5 .

## 4.2 Main Results Analysis (RQ1)

Table 1 shows that PRAISE achieves the best results on all five benchmarks in both F1 and EM, consistently outperforming prior baselines from agentic search, rollout reuse, and processsupervision categories. This gives a clear positive answer to RQ1 .

The gains over strong agentic search baselines such as R1-Searcher are substantial, especially on HotpotQA and 2Wiki, suggesting that PRAISE is particularly effective for multi-hop reasoning tasks that require sustained evidence accumulation across search turns. PRAISE also clearly outperforms PPO baselines with repeated updates on the same rollout data, indicating that its benefit comes not simply from more reuse, but from more effective reuse of informative prefixes. In addition, PRAISE surpasses process-supervision methods such as StepSearch, ReasonRAG, and TIPS, showing that reward assignment based on adjacent prefix gains provides a stronger training signal.

When joint optimization is removed and the policy model is directly reused as the evaluator without dedicated evaluator training, RL increasingly specializes the model toward the search objective, which can weaken its more general ability to assess prefix quality. ## 4.4 How Joint Optimization Shapes the Prefix Evaluator (RQ3)

To understand why joint optimization helps, we analyze the prefix evaluator at different search stages during training. We compare three settings: No-train evaluator (the frozen initial ref- erence model), Non-joint optimization (training the search policy without updating the evaluator), and Joint optimization (the full PRAISE framework). We cap the maximum number of search turns and measure the evaluator's answer F1 at Turn 0, Turn 1, and Turn 2. To make the advantage of joint optimization more explicit, we additionally report its step-wise F1 gain relative to the stronger of the other two settings, i.e., the higher F1 between non-joint optimization and the frozen evaluator at each step.

As shown in Figure 2, joint optimization consistently achieves the best performance across all prefix steps, while non-joint optimization remains below it and the frozen evaluator performs worst. This shows that the evaluator itself improves during training, and that such improvement is strongest when it is optimized jointly with the search policy.

without reward-based guidance. In contrast, positive values of α consistently improve performance over the no-process-reward setting, indicating that PRAISE is reasonably robust to the choice of α .

## 5 Conclusion

We presented PRAISE, a prefix-based rollout reuse framework for agentic search training. By converting intermediate prefixes into additional training samples and deriving step-level rewards from adjacent prefix gains, PRAISE improves both rollout utilization and credit assignment. Built on a sharedmodel design, it jointly optimizes search and prefix evaluation within a unified PPO framework, without requiring extra annotations or a separate reward model. Experiments on multi-hop QA benchmarks demonstrate consistent gains over strong baselines.

## A Appendix

## A.1 Discussion

- Why PPO instead of GRPO? We adopt PPO rather than GRPO for this setting. GRPO is natu-

rally designed for comparing a group of responses sampled from the same state. In multi-turn search, however, extending this paradigm would require grouped sampling at each turn and each intermediate state, leading to rapidly increasing rollout cost with the horizon. Although recent work such as IGPO extends a GRPO-style idea to multi-turn agents (Wang et al., 2025a), turns from different rollouts generally correspond to different states, making strict same-state grouping difficult to preserve. PPO is therefore a more natural choice for optimizing trajectories with token-level or turnlevel rewards in our setting.

Why not use likelihood-based prefix scoring? PRAISE also differs from likelihood-based prefix scoring methods such as TIPS (Xie et al., 2026) and IGPO (Wang et al., 2025a). Those methods estimate intermediate utility using the log-likelihood of the golden answer conditioned on a prefix. While this can serve as a zero-shot evaluation signal, it is less suitable for joint training. Once optimized directly, the objective becomes close to supervised fine-tuning on the reference answer, encouraging answer memorization rather than measuring the incremental value of newly retrieved evidence. By instead generating rollout-based prefix answers and scoring them with task reward, PRAISE preserves the meaning of process reward as the marginal gain brought by each search step.
